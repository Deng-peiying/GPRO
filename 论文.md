\begin{abstract}
Executable robot world models depend on three coupled ingredients: data that expose realistic interaction patterns, inverse dynamics that can stably recover control from visual change, and world-model rollouts that remain both embodied and controllable. Current video-to-action pipelines are often weak at one or more of these layers. Coverage-oriented collection can miss action-identifiable interaction transitions, RGB-dominant inverse dynamics can underuse spatial and kinematic cues, and generic video generators may either fail to represent robot embodiment and manipulation causality or remain weakly grounded for executable control even after supervised adaptation. We argue that these failures are best understood as a broader \emph{embodied action grounding} deficiency, with the executability gap as its downstream symptom. Based on this view, we make three contributions. First, using AnyPos-style task-agnostic collection as a controlled reference point in RoboTwin, we show that task-conditioned data (T/ST) supports action recovery on interaction-heavy test distributions much better than coverage-oriented data (U/S), with S and ST serving as controls that separate spatial bias from task-phase structure. The spatial prior is estimated from DROID trajectories to better match the Franka workspace. Second, we introduce a Grounded Inverse Dynamics Model (GIDM) that combines robot-region masking, depth prior, depth motion, and control-state context, and improves action recovery over AnyPos- and Vidar-style baselines under a matched protocol. Third, we adapt Wan2.1 with embodiment-aware supervised fine-tuning and FK-grounded state-unrolled GRPO, so that world-model adaptation targets both embodiment fidelity and control-grounded executability. Together, these results suggest that progress on executable robot world models requires jointly grounding data, action inference, and world-model adaptation in embodied manipulation structure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Video world models are becoming a practical substrate for robot planning because they expose a simple and general interface: predict what the robot should see next, then use the predicted future to choose actions. This interface has been explored in video-conditioned manipulation, inverse-dynamics control, and recent executable-alignment methods~\cite{unipi,susie,robodreamer,ardup,vidar,eva}, where an inverse dynamics model (IDM) translates generated visual rollouts into robot commands. The attraction is clear: a visual future can represent long-horizon task progress without requiring every downstream controller to learn its own task-specific latent space.

In practice, this pipeline depends on three coupled ingredients: data that contain realistic interaction transitions, an inverse-dynamics interface that can recover action from visual change, and a world model whose predicted futures remain both embodied and controllable. Weakness at any one of these layers can break the full system. Coverage-oriented data may describe motion without exposing contact-rich action patterns; RGB-dominant inverse dynamics may remain ambiguous when depth geometry or the current arm configuration matters; and generic video generators may either fail to represent the manipulator and operation causality or produce visually improved futures that still decode into unstable actions. In manipulation, many such failures are local and geometric: a gripper closes slightly before contact, an object floats after release, a placement is shifted in depth, or the generated arm motion implies a discontinuous joint update. Following EVA~\cite{eva}, we refer to this downstream control mismatch as the \emph{executability gap}.

Existing approaches attack different parts of this problem in isolation. AnyPos shows that task-agnostic automatic collection can scale robot action data without task-specific supervision~\cite{anypos}, but it leaves open a different question that matters for executable manipulation: whether coverage-oriented collection alone exposes enough contact-rich and success-relevant transitions for stable action recovery. Masked or direction-aware IDMs improve action decoding~\cite{vidar,stableidm}, but RGB-only inference remains ambiguous near contact and under visually similar transitions with different underlying robot states. Executability-aligned world models use IDM rewards~\cite{eva}, but such rewards are only as informative as the action interface and data distribution behind them. This leaves a broader systems question underexplored: what kinds of data, inverse dynamics, and world-model objectives actually make robot video planning controllable?

Our perspective is that these failures are all symptoms of a broader \emph{embodied action grounding gap}. The data layer determines whether the system ever observes realistic interaction structure, the IDM layer determines whether such structure can be decoded into stable control, and the world-model layer determines whether predicted futures preserve robot embodiment and remain usable for action recovery. We therefore organize the paper around three connected questions. First, inspired by AnyPos, we implement an AnyPos-style task-agnostic collection regime inside RoboTwin and use it as a controlled reference point for asking whether coverage-oriented collection is sufficient for executable manipulation. Second, we design an IDM that grounds action recovery in depth, robot-centric masking, and current control-state. Third, we adapt a video world model so that it is trained not only to look plausible, but also to preserve robot embodiment and support stable action recovery under closed-loop rollout.

Our empirical analysis follows the same structure. A controlled cross-evaluation benchmark shows that U/S reconstruct coverage-style motion well but fail badly on T/ST interaction-like distributions. In contrast, T/ST form the clear high-performing group on interaction-like evaluation, suggesting that task-agnostic coverage alone is a poor proxy for executable manipulation data.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make three contributions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em]
    \item We reframe executable robot world modeling as an embodied action grounding problem spanning data collection, inverse dynamics, and world-model adaptation, in which structural collapse and downstream executability failure arise from the same pipeline-level deficiency.
    \item We present a controlled empirical discussion of AnyPos-style task-agnostic collection by comparing four RoboTwin collection regimes (U, S, T, ST), using S and ST as controls to disentangle spatial bias from task-phase structure, and showing that task-conditioned regimes (T/ST) better support action recovery on interaction-heavy RoboTwin manipulation distributions than coverage-oriented U/S data.
    \item We introduce GIDM and an executable world-model adaptation pipeline based on embodiment-aware ST-SFT and FK-grounded state-unrolled GRPO, showing that stronger grounding improves both action recovery and downstream video-to-action execution.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Video world models and executable alignment.}
Video generation has become a common interface for robot planning because future frames can represent task progress in a form shared across objects, scenes, and embodiments. UniPi formulates policy learning as text-guided video generation followed by inverse-dynamics action extraction~\cite{unipi}. SuSIE synthesizes subgoal observations with an image-editing diffusion model~\cite{susie}, while RoboDreamer factorizes language-conditioned video generation into lower-level primitives for compositional generalization~\cite{robodreamer}. ARDuP argues that video planners should focus on active interaction regions rather than all pixels uniformly~\cite{ardup}. Vidar and Veo-Act extend this line with stronger embodied priors and hierarchical video-to-action pipelines~\cite{vidar,veoact}. EVA further introduces IDM-based reward alignment for executable robot video generation~\cite{eva}. These methods highlight the value of visual foresight, but most still optimize task or visual prediction quality rather than the executability of the induced control.

\paragraph{Inverse dynamics and robot data priors.}
IDMs provide the bridge from generated observations to robot actions. In video-based policies, the IDM often maps short-horizon observation pairs or generated rollouts into low-level control~\cite{unipi,ardup,vidar}. Predictive inverse-dynamics models such as Seer show that inverse dynamics can scale with large robot datasets and serve as strong vision-action learners~\cite{pidm}. AnyPos studies task-agnostic action data and introduces arm-decoupled and direction-aware IDM design for bimanual manipulation~\cite{anypos}. Vidar uses a masked inverse dynamics model to suppress distractors~\cite{vidar}, and StableIDM studies spatio-temporal refinement and robot-centric masking for robust action prediction under manipulator truncation~\cite{stableidm}. On the data side, DROID provides diverse in-the-wild manipulation trajectories~\cite{droid}, RoboTwin offers a scalable dual-arm simulation benchmark with task interfaces~\cite{robotwin}, and AnyPos/ATARA emphasizes task-agnostic exploration of feasible action space~\cite{anypos}. Our work connects these lines by using an AnyPos-style collection regime as a controlled reference, separating coverage-oriented and interaction-oriented data regimes, and grounding action recovery in depth and control-state rather than RGB alone.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Overview}

Throughout, we treat the low-dimensional robot signal as a controller-side control-state $\tilde{\mathbf{q}}_t$, and train the IDM on frame pairs separated by a fixed temporal gap $\Delta=4$ to predict the future control target $\mathbf{a}_t=\tilde{\mathbf{q}}_{t+\Delta}$. Our method follows a data--dynamics--world-model chain. The data layer determines whether realistic interaction patterns are available for learning. The dynamics layer determines whether those patterns can be decoded into stable control using geometry and embodiment context. The world-model layer determines whether predicted futures preserve robot embodiment and remain usable for action recovery. We address these layers with prior-aware collection, GIDM, and embodiment- plus executability-aware world-model adaptation.

\subsection{Prior-Aware Data Collection}

AnyPos shows that task-agnostic automatic collection can scale robot action data without task-specific supervision. We use this idea as the starting point for the data-layer study rather than as a straw-man baseline. Our question is downstream-specific: when the goal is executable manipulation rather than broad motion coverage, is task-agnostic collection alone sufficient to expose the interaction transitions needed for stable action recovery? To study this under controlled conditions, we implement an AnyPos-style collection regime inside RoboTwin and use U versus T as the primary contrast between task-agnostic coverage and task-conditioned interaction. S and ST are included as control regimes that separate spatial bias from task-phase structure.

\paragraph{Collection settings.}
We instantiate four RoboTwin data regimes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em]
    \item \textbf{U: Uniform.} A task-agnostic baseline inspired by AnyPos/ATARA-style collection. End-effector targets are uniformly sampled inside a workspace cube, without task interaction or success conditioning.
    \item \textbf{S: Spatial prior.} Uniform sampling biased by a spatial prior estimated from DROID trajectories to better adapt exploration to the Franka workspace. This prior moves exploration toward regions where real robot interaction is more likely.
    \item \textbf{T: Task prior.} Sampling through 50 RoboTwin task interfaces, which produces transitions tied to task execution phases rather than only workspace coverage.
    \item \textbf{ST: Spatial-task prior.} Task-prior sampling further biased by the same DROID-derived spatial prior. This setting combines explicit task-phase transitions with a spatial bias derived from real robot manipulation data.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Design rationale.}
U serves as a controlled AnyPos-style reference for task-agnostic coverage, and T introduces task interfaces and therefore explicit contact, release, and terminal transitions. S adds a spatial prior but remains interaction-agnostic, while ST adds the same spatial prior on top of task-conditioned sampling. We include S/ST not as additional proposed collection methods, but as controls for testing whether any gain comes from Franka-adapted workspace coverage alone or from the interaction structure induced by task execution. Importantly, all four regimes are built on the same RoboTwin platform with matched scene generators, objects, embodiments, and camera setup. The primary difference therefore lies in the induced action and interaction-transition distribution rather than scene content.

\subsection{Grounded Inverse Dynamics Model (GIDM)}

We formulate inverse dynamics as multi-modal action recovery from two observations separated by $\Delta$ frames:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t =
f_{\theta}(\mathbf{o}^{rgbd}_t, \tilde{\mathbf{q}}_t, \mathbf{o}^{rgbd}_{t+\Delta}).
\label{eq:idm}
\end{equation}
The model takes RGB-D observations at times $t$ and $t+\Delta$ together with the current control-state $\tilde{\mathbf{q}}_t$ and predicts a $16$-dimensional future control target. All RGB and depth inputs are resized to $518\times 518$. This design addresses the main ambiguity of RGB-only inverse dynamics: similar 2D appearance changes can correspond to different executable next states when depth geometry or the current arm configuration differs.

The first branch is a mask-guided RGB motion branch. A shared U-Net predicts a task-relevant foreground mask for each RGB frame, which is binarized with a straight-through threshold and applied to suppress background clutter. The mask branch is trained end-to-end only through the action regression loss, without external segmentation supervision, so the learned mask is optimized for action-relevant visual evidence rather than semantic segmentation accuracy. The masked RGB pair is then encoded by a shared ResNet$_{RGB}$, producing $512$-dimensional features $f_t^{rgb}$ and $f_{t+\Delta}^{rgb}$. We represent appearance motion by feature differencing,
\begin{equation}
\Delta f_{rgb} = f_{t+\Delta}^{rgb} - f_t^{rgb},
\end{equation}
which captures object-centric semantic change while suppressing static background variation.

The second branch is a depth geometry branch. The depth maps at $t$ and $t+\Delta$ are processed by a shared ResNet$_{Depth}$ to produce $256$-dimensional features. We keep the full depth map because it provides a stable global geometric prior. The feature $f_t^{dep}$ serves as a depth prior for the current scene, and a depth-motion feature is constructed by
\begin{equation}
\Delta f_{dep} = f_{t+\Delta}^{dep} - f_t^{dep},
\end{equation}
so that the model can jointly reason about scene layout and 3D temporal change.

The third branch is a state branch that embeds the current control-state with a two-layer MLP of size $16\!\rightarrow\!64\!\rightarrow\!128$, producing $f_{state}$. This injects proprioceptive context when similar visual transitions imply different next targets.

The final predictor concatenates the three branches into
\begin{equation}
f_{fused} = [\Delta f_{rgb}; f_t^{dep}; \Delta f_{dep}; f_{state}],
\end{equation}
whose dimension is $1152 = 512 + 256 + 256 + 128$. A three-layer MLP with size $1152\!\rightarrow\!512\!\rightarrow\!256\!\rightarrow\!16$ regresses the predicted future control target $\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t$. Overall, the IDM combines object-centric RGB motion, global depth geometry, depth-based motion cues, and robot state context into a single executable action interface.

The training objective is a weighted action regression loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{IDM}} =
\sum_{j=1}^{D} \lambda_j \, \ell(\hat{a}_{t,j}, a_{t,j}),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_j$ can emphasize gripper or task-critical degrees of freedom. We first train GIDM with native depth to learn a strong geometry-grounded inverse-dynamics interface. Because full-pipeline world-model rollout later relies on DA3-predicted depth, we optionally finetune GIDM on DA3-generated depth observations to reduce the geometry-domain gap between standalone IDM training and deployment-time usage. All compared IDMs use the same data splits and action parameterization. AnyPos and Vidar are RGB-based baselines; our model adds depth and current control-state, and the ablations isolate each cue.

\subsection{World Model Adaptation Under Two Grounding Gaps}

We use Wan2.1 image-to-video as the world-model backbone, i.e., a pretrained RGB video generator that conditions on an initial image and a text prompt. Given the current RGB observation and task prompt, the model predicts a future RGB rollout:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{o}}^{rgb}_{t+1:t+H} =
g_{\phi}(\mathbf{o}^{rgb}_t, p).
\label{eq:wan}
\end{equation}
At the world-model layer, the embodied action grounding gap appears as two distinct failures. The first is a structural embodiment failure: a generic image-to-video model may not stably represent the manipulator or the temporal-causal chain of operation, leading to arm omission, morphology hallucination, and drift. The second is a control-grounding failure: even visually improved futures may still decode into unstable or infeasible actions.

\paragraph{Embodiment-aware supervised adaptation.}
Manipulation videos require preserving the causal chain from arm entry to contact, manipulation, and release. In pilot runs, short-window or random-cut SFT was difficult to optimize and often produced structural failures such as arm omission, morphology collapse, and motion incoherence. We therefore use ST-SFT, a supervised fine-tuning stage on the spatial-task (ST) data with causally complete manipulation windows, before RL post-training: an entry-focused warm-up handles cases where the arm is absent in the initial frame, followed by full-horizon SFT with $49$-frame speedup-style windows.

Because the IDM consumes RGB-D transitions while Wan2.1 outputs RGB, we use a DA3-based pseudo-depth bridge during executability post-training, where DA3 is an off-the-shelf monocular depth estimator used to convert generated RGB frames into pseudo-depth maps. This creates a geometry-domain gap between standalone IDM benchmarking and full-pipeline deployment. We therefore distinguish three regimes throughout the paper: native-depth GIDM as an upper-bound action-recovery interface, DA3 zero-shot evaluation to measure the deployment gap, and DA3-finetuned GIDM as the deployment-adapted variant used for world-model post-training and inference.

\subsection{FK-Grounded GRPO for Configuration-Aware Executability}

While embodiment-aware SFT reduces structural failure, it does not guarantee control-grounded action recovery. We therefore post-train the world model for executability rather than visual fidelity. The world model first generates an RGB rollout, the depth backend supplies pseudo-depth, and the frozen IDM evaluates rollout pairs separated by the same temporal gap $\Delta$ used during IDM training. For each rewarded transition, the decoded action becomes the control-state proxy for the next step:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t =
f_{\theta}(\mathbf{o}^{rgbd}_t, \tilde{\mathbf{q}}_t, \hat{\mathbf{o}}^{rgbd}_{t+\Delta}),
\qquad
\tilde{\mathbf{q}}^{proxy}_{t+\Delta} = \hat{\mathbf{a}}_t.
\end{equation}
The proxy is not the true physical future state; it is the control-space state used consistently with the IDM action-label definition.

We define the post-training reward as a sum of four penalty groups, each tied to a specific executability failure. The feasibility group prevents invalid joint-space commands, the behavior-cloning (BC) group keeps generated rollouts aligned with the paired demonstration action, the stability group discourages jitter and IDM sensitivity, and the FK group enforces Cartesian constraints that are invisible to RGB plausibility alone:
\begin{equation}
R_t = R_t^{\text{feas}} + R_t^{\text{bc}} + R_t^{\text{stab}} + R_t^{\text{fk}},
\qquad R_t \le 0,
\end{equation}
where lower values indicate more severe violation. The grouped penalties are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
R_t^{\text{feas}}
&= \lambda_{\text{feas}}
\bigl(R_t^{\text{veto}} + R_t^{\text{limit}} + R_t^{\Delta c}\bigr), \\
R_t^{\text{bc}}
&= \lambda_{\text{bc}}
\bigl(-\|\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t-\mathbf{a}^{gt}_t\|_1\bigr), \\
R_t^{\text{stab}}
&= \lambda_{\text{stab}}
\bigl(R_t^{\text{trans}} + R_t^{\text{vel}} + R_t^{\text{acc}}
+ R_t^{\text{jerk}} + R_t^{\text{idm-var}}\bigr), \\
R_t^{\text{fk}}
&= \lambda_{\text{fk}}
\bigl(R_t^{\text{dual}} + R_t^{\text{ws}} + R_t^{\text{sing}}
+ R_t^{\text{cvel}} + R_t^{\text{cacc}} + R_t^{\text{chain}}\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The feasibility group contains three joint-space penalties: a hard veto $R_t^{\text{veto}}=-50\cdot \mathbf{1}[\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t\text{ non-finite}]$, a quadratic soft joint-limit penalty $R_t^{\text{limit}}=-10\cdot \mathrm{MSE}(\mathbf{b}_t^{\text{below}}+\mathbf{b}_t^{\text{above}})^2$, and a control-delta penalty $R_t^{\Delta c}=-\|\max(0,|\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t-\tilde{\mathbf{q}}_t|-0.25)\|_1$. The BC term is a standard L1 deviation from the aligned demonstration action $\mathbf{a}^{gt}_t$, which is available because post-training prompts and initial observations are sampled from paired RoboTwin demonstration trajectories with synchronized action labels.

The stability group combines first-order transition smoothness, finite-difference joint velocity/acceleration/jerk limits with
$\mathbf{v}_t=(\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t-\hat{\mathbf{a}}_{t-1})/dt$,
$\mathbf{u}_t=(\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t-2\hat{\mathbf{a}}_{t-1}+\hat{\mathbf{a}}_{t-2})/dt^2$, and
$\mathbf{j}_t=(\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t-3\hat{\mathbf{a}}_{t-1}+3\hat{\mathbf{a}}_{t-2}-\hat{\mathbf{a}}_{t-3})/dt^3$, plus an IDM perturbation-consistency term:
\begin{equation}
R_t^{\text{idm-var}} =
-\left\|
\mathrm{Var}_m\left[
f_{\theta}(\mathbf{o}^{rgbd}_t,\tilde{\mathbf{q}}_t,\mathcal{P}_m(\hat{\mathbf{o}}^{rgbd}_{t+\Delta}))
\right]
\right\|_1 .
\end{equation}
Finally, the FK-Cartesian group uses forward kinematics and the Jacobian to penalize dual-arm proximity below $5$cm, workspace violations outside $[0.15,0.855]$m from the base, singular configurations with condition number above $50$, Cartesian velocity above $1.7$ m/s, Cartesian acceleration above $10$ m/s$^2$, and FK-chain inconsistency when the end-effector displacement exceeds $v_{\max}dt$ (about $5.7$cm in our setup).

\subsection{State-Unrolled Flow-GRPO}

Flow-GRPO adapts group-relative policy optimization to a flow-matching video model by replacing token log-probabilities with a trajectory-level surrogate defined on the stored latent flow path. For each condition, we sample a group of $G$ candidate rollouts and compute cumulative reward:
\begin{equation}
R^{(k)} = \sum_{t=0}^{H-1} \eta_t R^{(k)}_t,
\qquad k=1,\dots,G.
\end{equation}
The group-relative advantage is
\begin{equation}
A^{(k)} = \frac{R^{(k)}-\mu_R}{\sigma_R+\epsilon}.
\end{equation}
For a sampled rollout $k$, let $\{(\mathbf{z}^{(k)}_s,\tau_s,\dot{\mathbf{z}}^{(k)}_s)\}_{s=1}^{S}$ denote the stored latent states, flow timesteps, and latent flow targets along the generation path. We define a surrogate trajectory score
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\ell}^{(k)}_{\phi}
= -\sum_{s=1}^{S}
\left\|
v_{\phi}(\mathbf{z}^{(k)}_s,\tau_s,c)
- \dot{\mathbf{z}}^{(k)}_s
\right\|_2^2,
\end{equation}
where $v_{\phi}$ is the current flow prediction and $c$ is the conditioning input. The clipped update uses
\begin{equation}
r^{(k)}_{\phi}
=
\exp\!\left(
\tilde{\ell}^{(k)}_{\phi}
- \tilde{\ell}^{(k)}_{\text{old}}
\right),
\end{equation}
and optimizes
\begin{align}
J_{\text{clip}}
&=
\frac{1}{G}\sum_{k=1}^{G}
\min\!\Big(
r^{(k)}_{\phi} A^{(k)},
\bar{r}^{(k)}_{\phi} A^{(k)}
\Big), \\
\bar{r}^{(k)}_{\phi}
&=
\mathrm{clip}\!\left(
r^{(k)}_{\phi},
1-\epsilon_{\text{clip}},
1+\epsilon_{\text{clip}}
\right), \\
D_{\text{ref}}
&=
\frac{1}{G}\sum_{k=1}^{G}\sum_{s=1}^{S}
\left\|
v_{\phi}(\mathbf{z}^{(k)}_s,\tau_s,c)
- v_{\text{ref}}(\mathbf{z}^{(k)}_s,\tau_s,c)
\right\|_2^2 .
\end{align}
The final loss is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{Flow-GRPO}}
= -J_{\text{clip}} + \beta D_{\text{ref}} .
\end{equation}
This objective is not a true video likelihood; it is a flow-space surrogate that lets GRPO compare current, old, and reference flow predictions on the same sampled latent paths. Rollout records store latent states, timesteps, old flow predictions, decoded videos, control-state recursions, and rewards; updates recompute current and reference flow predictions on the same paths.

\subsection{AnyPos-Style Data Collection Study}

Tables~\ref{tab:data_cross_eval_mae} and~\ref{tab:data_cross_eval_rmse} evaluate how far an AnyPos-style task-agnostic collection regime can support action recovery under held-out distributions. This cross-evaluation should be read as an operational test of train-test support for action decoding, rather than a direct measurement of intrinsic transition identifiability independent of distribution shift. Under this criterion, U and S achieve the lowest errors when both training and evaluation come from coverage-style distributions. However, these models do not transfer to T/ST test distributions: their MAE rises from below $0.005$ on U/S test sets to roughly $0.24$--$0.25$ on T/ST test sets. This result does not negate the value of task-agnostic collection for broad motion coverage; instead, it identifies where coverage-oriented data becomes insufficient for the interaction-heavy transitions needed in executable manipulation.

T and ST show the opposite behavior. Although they are not optimized for U/S coverage motion, they remain accurate on T/ST test sets and form the clear high-performing group on interaction-like distributions. In the MAE matrix, T/ST achieve only $0.0058$--$0.0088$ on T/ST test sets, whereas U/S remain around $0.2400$--$0.2538$. The RMSE matrix shows the same pattern, with T/ST at $0.0145$--$0.0397$ and U/S at $0.4313$--$0.4567$ on the same test sets. Since scene-level factors are controlled across the four regimes, this gap is unlikely to be explained by scene diversity alone; instead, it supports the narrower data claim that task-conditioned collection gives the IDM proxy better training support for action recovery on interaction-heavy manipulation transitions, while the spatial-only control S shows that spatial bias alone is not sufficient.

\subsection{Analysis}

The cross-evaluation matrix shows that U/S and T/ST induce different action distributions. U/S match free-space coverage trajectories, while T/ST expose the contact, release, and terminal phases needed by an executable world model. Under matched training counts and identical IDM proxy architecture, T/ST provide a much stronger training signal than U/S on interaction-like evaluation distributions. The relevant conclusion is therefore not that AnyPos-style task-agnostic collection is uninformative, but that executable manipulation benefits from adding interaction-structured task priors beyond coverage alone.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a pipeline view of executable robot world models centered on embodied action grounding. Our main claim is that failures in data coverage, action recovery, and world-model rollout are coupled rather than isolated, and that structural collapse and downstream executability failure arise from the same pipeline-level grounding deficiency. In RoboTwin, task-conditioned collection provides substantially stronger support for action recovery on interaction-like cross-evaluation settings than coverage-oriented U/S data. GIDM provides a stronger action-grounded recovery interface, while embodiment-aware supervised adaptation and FK-grounded state-unrolled GRPO move Wan2.1 toward robot-consistent and action-recoverable futures.
